### Part A: Driving Point Impedance

#### 1. Circuit in Fig. 1(a) - Parallel/Series Combination
**Given Parameters:** $R = 1.2\text{ k}\Omega$, $C_1 = 0.47\mu\text{F}$, $C_2 = 0.047\mu\text{F}$.
The driving point impedance $Z_{in}$ is the series combination of $C_1$ and the parallel pair of $R$ and $C_2$. The analytical expression is:
$$Z_{in} = Z_{C1} + \left( R \parallel Z_{C2} \right) = \frac{1}{j\omega C_1} + \frac{R \cdot \frac{1}{j\omega C_2}}{R + \frac{1}{j\omega C_2}} = -j\frac{1}{\omega C_1} + \frac{R - j\omega R^2 C_2}{1 + (\omega R C_2)^2}$$

Using angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$, we determine the magnitude $|Z_{in}|$ and phase $\angle Z_{in}$:

*   **At f = 500 Hz** ($\omega = 3141.6$ rad/s):
    $Z_{C1} = -j677.3\,\Omega$
    $Z_{p} (R \parallel C_2) = \frac{1200 \times (-j6772.6)}{1200 - j6772.6} = 1163.5 - j206.1\,\Omega$
    $Z_{in} = 1163.5 - j206.1 - j677.3 = 1163.5 - j883.4\,\Omega$
    **$|Z_{in}| = 1460.8\,\Omega$ , $\angle Z_{in} = -37.2^\circ$**

*   **At f = 2 kHz** ($\omega = 12566.4$ rad/s):
    $Z_{in} = 798.9 - j735.5\,\Omega$ $\Rightarrow$ **$|Z_{in}| = 1086.0\,\Omega$ , $\angle Z_{in} = -42.6^\circ$**

*   **At f = 4 kHz** ($\omega = 25132.7$ rad/s):
    $Z_{in} = 398.7 - j650.0\,\Omega$ $\Rightarrow$ **$|Z_{in}| = 762.5\,\Omega$ , $\angle Z_{in} = -58.5^\circ$**

*   **At f = 10 kHz** ($\omega = 62831.9$ rad/s):
    $Z_{in} = 88.5 - j347.5\,\Omega$ $\Rightarrow$ **$|Z_{in}| = 358.6\,\Omega$ , $\angle Z_{in} = -75.7^\circ$**

### Part B: Transfer Function (Voltage Gain)

**Given Parameters for Fig. 2 circuits:** $R = 5.1\text{ k}\Omega$, $C = 0.047\mu\text{F}$.
The time constant is $\tau = RC = (5100)(0.047 \times 10^{-6}) = 2.397 \times 10^{-4}\text{ s}$.
The theoretical **-3dB cutoff frequency** is:
$$f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} = \frac{1}{2\pi(2.397 \times 10^{-4})} \approx \mathbf{664.1 \text{ Hz}}$$

#### 1. Circuit in Fig. 2(a) - First-Order RC Low-Pass Filter
The transfer function $H(j\omega)$ across the capacitor is:
$$H(j\omega) = \frac{V_0}{V_s} = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC}$$
*   **Amplitude (dB):** $20 \log_{10}|H(j\omega)| = -10 \log_{10}(1 + (\omega RC)^2) \text{ dB}$
*   **Phase:** $\angle H(j\omega) = -\arctan(\omega RC)$
*(At $f_c = 664.1$ Hz, Amplitude is $-3\text{ dB}$, Phase is $-45^\circ$)*

#### 2. Circuit in Fig. 2(b) - First-Order RC High-Pass Filter
The transfer function $H(j\omega)$ across the resistor is:
$$H(j\omega) = \frac{V_0}{V_s} = \frac{j\omega RC}{1 + j\omega RC}$$
*   **Amplitude (dB):** $20 \log_{10}(\omega RC) - 10 \log_{10}(1 + (\omega RC)^2) \text{ dB}$
*   **Phase:** $\angle H(j\omega) = 90^\circ - \arctan(\omega RC)$
*(At $f_c = 664.1$ Hz, Amplitude is $-3\text{ dB}$, Phase is $+45^\circ$)*

## 4. Answers to Pre-Lab Questions

**Q1) While measuring the driving point impedance in Fig.1(b), is a small resistor needed to measure the current? Why?**
**Answer:** No, an additional small sensing resistor is not needed. In the series circuit of Fig. 1(b), the resistor $R$ ($510\Omega$) is already in series with the capacitor, and its bottom terminal is connected to the common ground. Since the current is the same throughout a series circuit, the total current can be determined directly by measuring the voltage drop across this existing resistor $R$ using the oscilloscope ($I = V_R / R$). Introducing an extra small resistor would be redundant and would unnecessarily increase the total impedance, causing a slight measurement error.

**Q2) In the measurement, how to determine the -3dB frequency?**
**Answer:** The -3dB frequency (cutoff frequency) is the frequency at which the output voltage amplitude drops to $1/\sqrt{2}$ (approximately **$0.707$**) of its maximum passband value. 
To determine this during the practical experiment:
1. First, ensure the input voltage $V_s$ from the Function Generator is strictly maintained at an amplitude of $1\text{V}$ (or another convenient constant).
2. For a low-pass filter, find the maximum output voltage at a very low frequency (it should be nearly $1\text{V}$). 
3. Gradually increase the frequency on the Function Generator while monitoring the output voltage on the oscilloscope (CH2).
4. Stop adjusting when the peak-to-peak or RMS voltage on CH2 drops to exactly $0.707 \times V_{max}$. The frequency displayed on the generator at this exact moment is the practical -3dB frequency.

**Q3) While measuring the driving point impedances, use the Digital Multi-meter to replace the Digital Oscilloscope in Fig.1(a) and (b). Are there any differences in the use of two instruments?**
**Answer:** Yes, there are significant differences between using a Digital Multi-meter (DMM) and a Digital Oscilloscope in this AC context:
1. **Phase Measurement Capability:** A Digital Oscilloscope can display both the voltage and current waveforms simultaneously (via CH1 and CH2), allowing us to measure the time delay ($\Delta t$) or use the Lissajous figure (ellipse method) to calculate the phase difference ($\theta$). A standard DMM can only measure scalar magnitudes (RMS voltage/current) and cannot measure phase differences at all.
2. **Frequency Bandwidth Limitation:** Most standard laboratory DMMs are calibrated to provide accurate True RMS readings only at lower frequencies (typically 50 Hz to 400 Hz). At higher frequencies (e.g., the 5 kHz and 10 kHz required in this lab), the DMM's internal circuitry will attenuate the signal, leading to highly inaccurate magnitude readings. An oscilloscope, however, has a high bandwidth (usually 50 MHz or more) and maintains high amplitude accuracy across the entire frequency range used in this experiment.
