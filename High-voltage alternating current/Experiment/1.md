## 3. Theoretical Background & Adjusted Equations
### 3.1 Driving Point Impedance (Fig 1)
Driving point impedance is defined as the ratio of phasor voltage to its current at the same port: $Z = \mathbf{V} / \mathbf{I}$.
*   **Fig 1(a) Parallel-Series Circuit**: 
    $$Z_{1a} = Z_{C1} + (R \parallel Z_{C2}) = \frac{1}{j\omega C_1} + \frac{R \cdot \frac{1}{j\omega C_2}}{R + \frac{1}{j\omega C_2}}$$
*   **Fig 1(b) Series Circuit**: 
    $$Z_{1b} = R + \frac{1}{j\omega C}$$
    *   **Adjusted Theoretical Calculation (e.g., at $f = 2\text{ kHz}$)**: 
        Using our measured actual components ($R = 509\text{ }\Omega$, $C = 0.1\text{ }\mu\text{F}$):
        $$Z_{1b(2kHz)} = 509 - j \frac{1}{2\pi (2000)(0.1 \times 10^{-6})} = 509 - j 795.8 \text{ }\Omega$$
        **Magnitude**: $|Z| = \sqrt{509^2 + (-795.8)^2} \approx 944.6\text{ }\Omega$
        **Phase Angle**: $\phi = \arctan\left(\frac{-795.8}{509}\right) \approx -57.4^\circ$

### 3.2 Filter Transfer Functions & Cutoff Frequency (Fig 2)
The transfer function $H(j\omega) = V_{out}/V_{in}$ characterizes the frequency domain behavior.
*   **Low-pass Filter**: $H_{LP}(j\omega) = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC}$. It allows low frequencies to pass while attenuating high frequencies.
*   **High-pass Filter**: $H_{HP}(j\omega) = \frac{j\omega RC}{1 + j\omega RC}$. It blocks low frequencies and allows high frequencies to pass.
*   **Adjusted Cutoff Frequency ($f_c$)**: Defined as the point where the voltage ratio drops to $1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707$ ($-3\text{dB}$). Using our actual measured components ($R = 5.08\text{ k}\Omega$, $C = 0.045\text{ }\mu\text{F}$), the precise theoretical cutoff is:
    $$f_{c(adjusted)} = \frac{1}{2\pi (5080)(0.045 \times 10^{-6})} \approx 696.2 \text{ Hz}$$

### 3.3 Phase Measurement Methodology
Two methods were theoretically prepared and practically applied:
1.  **Dual-Trace Time Difference Method**: Phase shift $\phi (\text{deg}) = \frac{\Delta t}{T} \times 360^\circ = \Delta t \times f \times 360^\circ$.
2.  **Lissajous Ellipse Method (XY Mode)**: $\phi = \arcsin(B/A)$, where $A$ is the maximum vertical amplitude and $B$ is the vertical intercept at the horizontal center.

## 6. Comprehensive Error and Phenomenon Analysis
1.  **Cutoff Frequency Shift ($664\text{ Hz}$ vs $700\text{ Hz}$)**: 
    The nominal theoretical $f_c$ is $664.5\text{ Hz}$. However, our experimental $-3\text{dB}$ points were located at exactly $700\text{ Hz}$ (Low-pass) and $710\text{ Hz}$ (High-pass). This apparent "error" is entirely mathematically justified by our Component Calibration (Section 5.1). The actual capacitor had a $-4.26\%$ tolerance ($0.045\text{ }\mu\text{F}$ instead of $0.047\text{ }\mu\text{F}$). Plugging this into the formula yields an *adjusted theoretical cutoff* of $696.2\text{ Hz}$. The experimental values of $700/710\text{ Hz}$ reflect an exceptional accuracy with less than $2\%$ relative error compared to the adjusted reality.
2.  **Passband Gain Exceeding 1 (or 0 dB)**: 
    In the passband (e.g., $5\text{ kHz}$ to $20\text{ kHz}$ for the high-pass filter), the measured voltage ratio slightly exceeded 1.0 (e.g., $1.051$). Theoretically, a passive RC network cannot amplify signals. This phenomenon is a well-documented artifact caused by the oscilloscope's internal parasitic capacitance parallel to the circuit at high frequencies, as well as minor resonance effects introduced by the jumper wires on the breadboard.
3.  **Phase Behavior**: 
    The theoretical phase shift at the cutoff frequency should strictly be $-45^\circ$ for a low-pass and $+45^\circ$ for a high-pass filter. Our recorded values ($-44.4^\circ$ at $700\text{ Hz}$ and $45.5^\circ$ at $710\text{ Hz}$) represent a near-perfect empirical validation of linear control theory.

## 7. Answers to Questions

**1) While measuring the driving point impedance in Fig.1(b), is a small resistor needed to measure the current? Why?**
**Answer:** Yes, a small series sampling resistor ($R_{sample}$) is strictly necessary. An oscilloscope is fundamentally a voltage-measuring instrument with a very high input impedance (typically $1\text{ M}\Omega$) and cannot measure current directly. By introducing $R_{sample}$, we convert the current signal into a proportional voltage signal $V_R$, allowing calculation via Ohm's Law ($I = V_R / R_{sample}$). The resistor must be very small (e.g., $10\text{ }\Omega$) to minimize its interference with the total driving point impedance. Furthermore, the ground clips of the oscilloscope probes must both be attached to the negative terminal of this resistor to establish a common ground and prevent short circuits.

**2) In the measurement, how to determine the -3dB frequency?**
**Answer:** The $-3\text{dB}$ frequency (cutoff frequency $f_c$) is determined experimentally by scanning the input frequency and observing the corresponding output voltage $V_{out}$. Mathematically, $-3\text{dB}$ represents the half-power point, where the voltage drops to $1/\sqrt{2}$ (or $\approx 0.707$) of the input voltage $V_{in}$. During the lab, while keeping the input $V_{in}$ strictly monitored (e.g., $2.03\text{ V}$), we carefully tuned the frequency knob on the generator until $V_{out}$ reached $0.707 \times V_{in}$ (which is approximately $1.43\text{ V}$), and subsequently recorded that specific frequency point.

**3) While measuring the driving point impedances, use the Digital Multi-meter to replace the Digital Oscilloscope in Fig.1(a) and (b). Are there any differences in the use of two instruments?**
**Answer:** There are profound differences in both capability and frequency response. 
First, a Digital Multi-meter (DMM) can only measure the RMS scalar values of AC voltage and current. Thus, it can only yield the *magnitude* of the impedance ($|Z|$). It provides no temporal data, making it completely impossible to measure the time delay ($\Delta t$) or phase shift ($\phi$) between voltage and current.
Second, standard laboratory DMMs suffer from severe bandwidth limitations. They are optimized for low mains frequencies (50Hz - 400Hz) and display immense attenuation and errors at higher frequencies like $10\text{ kHz}$. Conversely, an oscilloscope possesses a high bandwidth (typically $>50\text{ MHz}$), enabling precise simultaneous observation of both magnitude and phase dynamics across the entire tested spectrum.
