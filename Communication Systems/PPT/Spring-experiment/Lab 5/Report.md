## 1. Objective
The primary objective of this laboratory is to design and implement a robust Voice Activity Detection (VAD) system. The system's goal is to effectively separate genuine human speech utterances from silence and complex background noise. Accurate endpoint detection is a critical preprocessing step in speech recognition and telecommunications, as it reduces computational load and prevents noise from being processed as speech. 

By analyzing audio signals in short, overlapping frames, we extract two fundamental acoustic features: Short-Time Energy (STE) and Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR). Using adaptive statistical thresholding, logical combinations, and median filter-based morphological smoothing, the experiment aims to accurately identify the absolute start and end boundaries of utterances across both a clean acoustic environment ("Harvard_sentences.wav") and a highly degraded, noisy channel ("moon.wav").

## 2. Theoretical Background
Human speech is a non-stationary signal, meaning its statistical properties continuously change. However, over very short durations (typically 20–30 ms), the vocal tract geometry remains relatively constant. Thus, speech is treated as "quasi-stationary," justifying the use of short-time frame analysis.

### 2.1 Short-Time Energy (STE)
Voiced speech sounds (such as vowels /a/, /i/, /u/ and nasals) are produced by the periodic vibration of the vocal cords. These sounds carry the majority of speech energy and exhibit large amplitudes. The STE for a given frame $i$ of length $N$ is defined as the sum of squared sample values:
$$E_i = \sum_{n=1}^{N} [x(n)]^2$$
STE is an excellent indicator for locating the core vocalic segments of a word, but it usually fails to detect low-energy consonants.

### 2.2 Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR)
Unvoiced sounds (such as fricatives /s/, /f/ and stops /p/, /t/, /k/) are generated by forcing air through a constricted vocal tract without vocal cord vibration. This creates turbulent airflow that resembles high-frequency white noise. These sounds have very low energy (making STE ineffective) but cross the zero-amplitude axis rapidly. The ZCR is mathematically defined as:
$$ZCR_i = \frac{1}{2N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} |sgn[x(n)] - sgn[x(n-1)]|$$
ZCR is crucial for identifying the onset (beginning) and offset (ending) of words, which are often composed of unvoiced consonants.

## 3. Methodology and Procedure
The VAD algorithm was implemented in MATLAB through the following systematic pipeline:

1.  **Signal Pre-processing:** 
    *   The audio signals were converted to mono to simplify multi-channel processing.
    *   **DC Offset Removal:** $x = x - mean(x)$ was applied to center the waveform precisely at zero, preventing bias in ZCR calculations.
    *   **Normalization:** The amplitude was scaled by $x / max(|x|)$ to bound the signal within $[-1, 1]$, ensuring the thresholding logic remains consistent regardless of the original microphone gain.
2.  **Framing Strategy:** 
    *   The `buffer` function segmented the signal into frames of $25\text{ ms}$. 
    *   A **50% overlap** was utilized. Overlapping guarantees that a transient acoustic event (like a short plosive burst) occurring at the boundary of a frame is not arbitrarily split or overlooked by the feature extraction window.
3.  **Adaptive Feature Extraction:** 
    *   STE and ZCR were computed for each frame.
    *   Instead of hard-coded thresholds, an **Adaptive Thresholding** method was employed to measure the baseline room tone. The first 10 frames were assumed to be background noise.
    *   Thresholds were dynamically computed using the Gaussian statistical model: $Threshold = \mu_{noise} + k \times \sigma_{noise}$ (where $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of the first 10 frames).
4.  **Mask Refinement (VAD Logic):** 
    *   STE and ZCR binary masks were combined via a Logical OR operation.
    *   A Median Filter (`medfilt1`) with a large window size (Order = 31) was applied to the combined mask to eliminate rapid toggling ("chatter") and output a continuous speech envelope.

## 5. Code Implementation & Threshold Discussion

### 5.1 Deep Dive into Threshold Multipliers ($k$ values)
The selection of the multiplier $k$ dictates the system's strictness and sensitivity. It balances the trade-off between False Rejection (missing actual speech) and False Acceptance (classifying noise as speech).

*   **For "Harvard_sentences.wav" ($k_{STE} = 2$, $k_{ZCR} = 2$):** 
    Because the room tone is stable, the standard deviation ($\sigma$) of the first 10 frames is extremely small. A $k$ value of 2 sets a threshold at the $95\%$ confidence interval of the background noise. This low multiplier was sufficient to capture even quiet whispers while cleanly ignoring the silent background.
*   **For "moon.wav" ($k_{STE} = 5$, $k_{ZCR} = 3$):** 
    The space communication channel is dominated by random static. The noise here is highly erratic and non-Gaussian. Using a small $k$ would result in the ZCR threshold being constantly pierced by the static's high-frequency nature. To counter this, $k_{STE}$ was increased to 5 and $k_{ZCR}$ to 3. This aggressively raised the decision boundary, forcing the algorithm to ignore the baseline hiss. Only the genuine, forceful unvoiced consonants (which exhibit a ZCR even higher than the static) and the loud vowels were permitted to cross this elevated threshold.

## 6. Analysis Questions

**Question: Describe how the logical OR operation and the Median Filter improved the detection accuracy.**

**1. The Necessity of the Logical OR Operation:**
A robust VAD system cannot rely on a single acoustic feature because speech is phonetically diverse. 
*   If we *only* used STE, the system would aggressively cut off the beginnings and ends of words (e.g., the /s/ in "six"), resulting in severely clipped and unnatural audio. 
*   If we *only* used ZCR, the system would capture the consonants but would fail and drop to zero during the stable, low-frequency sustained vowels (e.g., the /I/ in "six").
*   By combining the features with a **Logical OR ($Mask_{STE} \lor Mask_{ZCR}$)**, we achieve a synergistic effect. STE secures the high-energy vocalic core, while ZCR acts as the "antennae," capturing the low-energy leading and trailing consonants. This ensures the full phonological envelope of the utterance is preserved.

**2. The Role of the Median Filter (Mask Refinement):**
Even with the OR operation, the raw combined mask is highly fragmented. Human speech naturally contains microscopic pauses, such as the complete silence during the occlusion phase of stop consonants (e.g., the brief breath-hold before pronouncing /p/, /t/, or /k/). 
*   These syllabic gaps cause the instantaneous features to plummet below the threshold, resulting in "chatter"—rapid toggling between 1 (Speech) and 0 (Silence) within a single spoken word. 
*   Applying a **Median Filter** with a substantial window size (e.g., an order of 31 frames, which translates to roughly $380\text{ ms}$ of temporal smoothing) solves this. The median filter acts as a morphological closing operator: it bridges these tiny internal gaps and smooths out isolated noise spikes. Consequently, it transforms the fragmented, chaotic binary array into solid, contiguous blocks of active speech, vastly improving the practical accuracy and usability of the endpoint detection.
